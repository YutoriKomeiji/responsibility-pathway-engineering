# Public Adoption and Fork Strategy

This note records the public adoption strategy for Responsibility Pathway Engineering.

Strategy name: Core-first, Template-led Adoption.

It is a strategy note only. It is not a marketing launch, publication record, endorsement request, certification claim, conformance claim, production approval, implementation announcement, standardization claim, legal review, safety review, compliance review, fairness review, social acceptance proof, or AI final-responsibility transfer mechanism.

## Core-first

Responsibility Pathway Engineering should remain one coherent product before it becomes a collection of slogans, templates, articles, experiments, or future implementation ideas.

The core product should preserve:

- responsibility pathway definitions
- human or institutional return points
- evidence logging
- approval gates and approval-skip boundaries
- repair, dispute, or reopening paths
- examples and checker boundaries
- non-claims and deferred capabilities
- construction-status labels for unfinished parts

## Template-led adoption

Adoption should begin with small useful artifacts that readers can copy, inspect, fork, or adapt.

The first public adoption surface should be practical:

- small YAML templates
- small examples
- small checklists
- small reader paths
- small bounded checker ideas

These should be useful without requiring the reader to understand the whole theory first.

## Why this balance matters

If marketing comes before the product core, the repository may become scattered or overclaimed.

If the product core waits for perfect completion before any public usefulness appears, the project may become invisible.

The intended balance is:

1. keep the core coherent
2. publish Level 2 repository walkthrough when reader paths are ready
3. provide a copyable lightweight template
4. invite stars, forks, and feedback through usefulness rather than hype
5. defer stronger review, implementation, conformance, or standardization language

## Star and fork posture

Stars and forks are valuable when they indicate that readers find the project useful, inspectable, and adaptable.

Do not seek stars or forks by implying maturity the repository does not have.

Good adoption signals:

- a reader copies a template
- a reader forks to adapt a responsibility-path record
- a reviewer comments on clarity or boundary risk
- a maintainer reuses a non-claim boundary
- a team experiments with human-return-point logging

Weak or risky adoption signals:

- attention based on overclaiming
- confusion between template and implementation
- confusion between checker pass and correctness proof
- confusion between repository walkthrough and certification
- forks that remove responsibility boundaries without replacing them

## Initial public message

A safe lightweight message is:

AI-assisted work needs records that can return to a human or institution.

RPE provides lightweight responsibility-path templates and review paths for AI-assisted workflows, without claiming certification, production readiness, legal validity, safety proof, compliance proof, social acceptance proof, or AI final-responsibility transfer.

## First adoption artifact

The first adoption artifact should be a copyable template for AI-assisted work responsibility paths.

It should be small enough to try quickly and structured enough to preserve:

- source reference
- actor or requester
- AI assistance boundary
- reviewer or approver
- evidence reference
- uncertainty or missing context
- human or institutional return point
- repair, dispute, or reopening path
- non-claim boundary

## Relation to Level 2

Level 2 publication may introduce this repository as a coherent public specification project with useful lightweight parts.

Level 2 should not claim external review completion, implementation maturity, conformance readiness, certification readiness, or production readiness.
