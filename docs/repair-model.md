# Repair Model

## Purpose

The Repair Model describes how a broken, obscured, or suspended Responsibility Pathway can be restored enough for explanation, correction, rollback, compensation, learning, or closure.

Repair does not mean that harm disappears.
Repair does not mean blame has been resolved.
Repair means that the pathway becomes actionable again.

## Minimal Definition

Repair is the process of restoring a Responsibility Pathway from a broken or unclear state to a state where an authorized entity can understand, judge, correct, compensate, continue, or close the pathway.

## Repair Triggers

Repair may be required when:

- context was lost
- authority was unclear
- evidence was incomplete
- a stop point failed
- responsibility could not return
- a cost was displaced
- an affected party became visible only after the fact
- an assumption failed
- an automated action exceeded intended scope
- the pathway reached a suspended state without a return point

## Repair Steps

A repair process may include:

1. Detect the break.
2. Preserve available evidence.
3. Identify the last reliable node.
4. Identify the missing or damaged pathway segment.
5. Return the state to an authorized entity.
6. Decide whether to continue, stop, rollback, compensate, or redesign.
7. Record the repair action.
8. Update the pathway so the same break is less likely to recur.

## Repair Roles

Repair may involve:

- affected party
- decision owner
- repair owner
- evidence custodian
- stop authority
- system operator
- organization representative
- external reviewer

## Repair Outcomes

A repair process may result in:

- explanation
- correction
- rollback
- compensation
- escalation
- process redesign
- evidence improvement
- formal closure
- unresolved status with documented reason

## Boundary

Repair is not a guarantee of justice, safety, legal compliance, or full recovery.

It is an engineering model for preserving and restoring responsibility pathways after interruption, ambiguity, or failure.
