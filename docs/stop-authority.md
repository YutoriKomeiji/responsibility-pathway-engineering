# Stop Authority

## Purpose

Stop Authority describes who or what can interrupt, suspend, reject, slow down, or prevent a Responsibility Pathway from continuing when continuation would exceed safe, authorized, meaningful, or reviewable conditions.

Stopping is not failure by default.
Stopping can be a responsibility-preserving action.

## Minimal Definition

Stop Authority is the recognized capability to pause, block, reverse, escalate, or refuse a pathway transition before responsibility becomes unreturnable or harm becomes harder to repair.

## Stop Actions

A Stop Authority may support:

- pause
- reject
- require review
- escalate
- slow down
- rollback
- revoke authorization
- disable execution
- quarantine evidence
- trigger repair process

## Required Conditions

A stop authority should be visible in the pathway:

- who can stop
- what can be stopped
- when stopping is allowed or required
- what evidence is needed
- what happens after stopping
- whether refusal or escalation is protected
- how stopped state returns to a human or authorized process

## Stop Failure Modes

Stop authority fails when:

- nobody has authority to stop
- authority exists but is hidden
- stopping is punished
- stop conditions are unclear
- the system continues faster than review is possible
- evidence needed to justify stopping is unavailable
- stopping is technically possible but organizationally impossible
- stopping creates cost for the wrong entity

## Relationship to Return Point

A stop point should usually connect to a return point.

Stopping without a return point may create suspension without repair.

A strong stop authority answers not only "who can stop?" but also "where does responsibility return after stopping?"

## Boundary

Stop Authority is not a claim that stopping is always correct.

It is a model for preserving the possibility of responsible interruption when continuation would make responsibility harder to understand, return, or repair.
